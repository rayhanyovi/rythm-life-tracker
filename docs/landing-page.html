01

Marketing Strategy Brief

The landing page should market relief and clarity, not feature abundance. The pitch is strongest when it feels adult, calm, and specific.

Positioning Statement

Rythm helps people keep recurring commitments without the noise and pressure of aggressive habit apps. It gives them one calm daily view, clear structure for the routines that matter, and enough Activity Log review to stay honest when life shifts.

Target Audience

Primary - "The habit app dropout"

This person has tried habit trackers, to-do apps, Notion templates, or paper systems. They are not lazy. They are exhausted by tools that either feel childish, overbearing, or too broad to support real daily follow-through.

  • Often a professional, founder, freelancer, student, or parent juggling multiple recurring commitments.
  • Needs structure, but resists apps that feel like games or dashboards.
  • Wants a short daily ritual, not a second job.

Secondary - "The self-directed operator"

This person wants a personal operating rhythm across work, health, money, and reflection. They care less about motivation and more about consistency.

Tertiary - "The reset season user"

Someone rebuilding routines after burnout, a move, a life transition, or a difficult season. They need calm structure more than intensity.

The Angle

  • Not another habit tracker. That is the headline-level contrast visitors should feel immediately.
  • Period-aware, not guilt-driven. Daily, weekly, monthly, and one-time tasks should feel like real rhythm instead of one giant streak game.
  • Quietly structured. The page should appeal to people who want a serious tool for personal consistency.
  • Enough accountability, not performance theater. Notes, streaks, and Activity Log review exist for reflection and correction, not dopamine farming.

Tone of Voice

What it should sound like

Calm, clear, adult, grounded, supportive, competent.

What it should not sound like

Hype-y, productivity-bro, spiritual fluff, startup-slick, childish, "crush your goals" language.

Copy test

If the sentence sounds like it belongs in a fitness challenge app, rewrite it.

The Three-Test Framework

5-second test: Does the hero explain what Rythm is and why it exists? The visitor should understand: this is a calmer system for recurring commitments.
30-second test: Can a visitor understand how it works after one scroll? They should see Today, Structure, Activity Log, and the contrast with noisy habit apps.
2-minute test: Does the page feel credible enough to try? Placeholder proof, comparison table, FAQ, and pricing structure should answer most objections even before real data exists.

Conversion Goal

  • Primary CTA: Start free
  • Secondary CTA: See how it works
  • Tertiary CTA: Join the early access list / Talk to us later if pricing or roadmap questions matter
  • CTA repetition: sticky nav, hero, after workflow section, after pricing, final CTA band

§14 - FAQ
FAQ
Neutralize the main objections without sounding defensive.
Is this just another habit tracker?

No. Rythm is built around recurring life commitments, not aggressive streak pressure or social gamification mechanics. It does use light progress signals — XP and badges as honest milestones — but they stay quiet and factual, never push-notification urgency or social comparison. It sits closer to a calm personal operating rhythm than a challenge app.

Can I use it for work and personal routines together?

Yes. The habit-list model is deliberately broad, so you can hold Health, Focus, Finance, Relationships, or Work in one structure without turning it into a project board.

Do I need to write notes every day?

No. Notes are optional and tied to completions. The product should still feel fast when you only want to check something done and move on.

Does it work on mobile?

Yes. The product is designed mobile-first and can be installed as a PWA so the daily ritual works on your phone without a separate app stack.

Does it work offline?

Partially. The shell can reopen offline, but live writes and Activity Log updates still need a connection. The landing page should state that honestly.

Can I import my current habits from another tool?

Not in the current product scope yet. For launch, the strongest path is a quick manual setup or a starter structure. Import can become a roadmap item later.

Is there a team or household plan?

Not in the current product scope. Rythm is designed as a personal system first. If a shared version matters later, that should be a separate expansion, not a vague promise.

Can I export my data?

Placeholder answer for now: export should exist on a paid tier, but the exact format and scope can be finalized later.

What will pricing actually be?

Still being finalized. The pricing section in this document is a placeholder anchor so the page has a believable commercial frame before market validation is complete.

Who is Rythm not for?

It is probably not for people who want a highly social, aggressively gamified, or enterprise-style productivity tool. Rythm has light gamification (XP, milestone badges) but it is quiet and factual — if you want a streak leaderboard or notification pressure loop, this is not that. The product is strongest when the goal is calm personal consistency.

Metrics to monitor

  • Primary CTA click-through rate - does the hero convert interest into action?

  • Click-through on "See how it works" - is the educational CTA doing its job?
  • Sign-up completion rate - does the promise on the page match the onboarding reality?
Pragmatic recommendation: because pricing is still uncertain, the safest live version may be: hero + workflow + screenshots + waitlist or early access CTA first. But this placeholder pricing section is still worth designing now, because it forces the value ladder to become concrete.
